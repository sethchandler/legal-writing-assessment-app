# Feedback Structure Guide

## Overall Format Requirements

- Write in clear, professional prose
- Use specific text citations when referencing the student's work
- Maintain constructive tone throughout
- Balance criticism with recognition of strengths

## Required Sections and Subsections

### 1. Executive Summary (3-4 sentences)
Brief overview that:
- States which framework was used (IRAC or CREAC)
- Identifies 1-2 strongest aspects
- Identifies 1-2 priority improvements needed
- Sets constructive tone for detailed feedback

### 2. Substantive Analysis Assessment

#### Legal Accuracy
- Correctness of rule statements
- Accuracy of legal principles applied
- Comparison to model answer's legal content

#### Issue Coverage
- Which issues/claims were properly identified
- Which were missed or inadequately addressed
- Depth of analysis for each issue

#### Application of Law to Facts
- Quality of factual analysis
- Strength of legal reasoning
- Use of relevant facts vs. irrelevant details

### 3. Structural and Organizational Assessment

#### Framework Execution
- How well the chosen framework (IRAC/CREAC) was implemented
- Consistency in following the framework
- Any deviations and their impact

#### Component Analysis
Brief assessment of each component:
- **Issue/Opening Conclusion**: Format and clarity
- **Rule**: Completeness, accuracy, organization
- **Explanation** (if CREAC): Quality of case illustrations
- **Application**: Fact-to-law connections, conclusory statements
- **Conclusion**: Appropriateness and completeness

#### Organizational Flow
- Logical progression of arguments
- Alignment between Rule and Application sections
- Transitions between issues/sections

### 4. Specific Strengths
Bullet points with examples:
- Quote specific well-written passages
- Identify particularly strong analysis
- Note effective use of legal writing techniques

### 5. Priority Areas for Improvement
Organized from most to least important:
- Each item should include:
  - The issue identified
  - Specific example from text
  - Why it matters
  - Constructive suggestion for improvement

### 6. Grade Assessment (if requested)

#### When Numeric Grade Requested
```
Suggested Grade: [X]/100

Breakdown:
- Substantive Analysis: [X]/[total available]
- Structural Organization: [X]/[total available]  
- Legal Writing Mechanics: [X]/[total available]
[Additional categories as specified in rubric]

Grade Justification: [2-3 sentences explaining the overall grade based on strengths and weaknesses identified above]
```

#### When Letter Grade Requested
```
Suggested Grade: [Letter Grade]

This grade reflects:
- [Primary strength influencing grade]
- [Primary weakness influencing grade]
- [Overall performance relative to expectations]
```

#### When Rubric Categories Provided
Use the exact categories and point values from the rubric:
```
Rubric Assessment:
- [Category 1 from rubric]: [X]/[Y] points
  Brief justification: [1 sentence]
- [Category 2 from rubric]: [X]/[Y] points
  Brief justification: [1 sentence]
[Continue for all rubric categories]

Total: [X]/[Y] points
```

### 7. Concluding Encouragement (2-3 sentences)
- Acknowledge effort and progress shown
- Reinforce key takeaway for improvement
- End on constructive, forward-looking note

## Formatting Guidelines

### Text References
When citing student's text:
- Use quotation marks for short phrases
- Use block quotes for longer passages (3+ lines)
- Include location reference (e.g., "In your Application section regarding negligence...")

### Comparison to Model Answer
When referencing model answer:
- "The model answer demonstrates..."
- "Compare your approach to the model's treatment of..."
- "Note how the model answer addresses..."

### Constructive Language Patterns

**Instead of:** "You failed to..."
**Use:** "Consider adding..." or "This could be strengthened by..."

**Instead of:** "This is wrong"
**Use:** "This interpretation differs from the established rule, which states..."

**Instead of:** "Poor organization"
**Use:** "The organization could be clarified by..."

## Special Situations

### When No Grade Requested
- Omit Section 6 entirely
- Focus on qualitative feedback
- May include general performance level (e.g., "This represents strong/competent/developing work")

### When Student Performance is Weak
- Start with something done correctly, even if minor
- Frame improvements as "opportunities for growth"
- Provide specific, actionable steps for improvement
- Maintain encouraging tone in conclusion

### When Student Performance is Strong
- Still identify areas for refinement
- Acknowledge excellence specifically
- Suggest advanced techniques for further development
- Avoid generic praise

### Batch Grading Mode
When evaluating multiple answers:
- Maintain consistent standards across all students
- Note common patterns across submissions if relevant
- Avoid comparing students to each other
- Treat each evaluation independently

## Length Guidelines

- **Brief feedback** (when requested): 300-500 words total
- **Standard feedback**: 500-800 words total
- **Detailed feedback** (when requested): 800-1200 words total
- **Comprehensive feedback with grade justification**: 1000-1500 words total

Adjust length based on complexity of issues identified and specific requirements, but avoid overwhelming detail that obscures key points.
